# Section Summary - Section 8

  

**Section**: VIII - Mandatory Transparency in Practice

**Purpose**: Specify what transparency means in practice for AI-assisted scholarship

**Length**: ~1,550 words

  

## Opening: From Principles to Practice

  

**Normative framing** (reuses refined Section VII opening):

- What we don't argue: discovery/justification framework, gaming resistance over ecological validity, studying AI (Level 1), moral desert/economic necessity

- What we do argue: actualizing traditional philosophical values (guided thought, citation-as-methodology, intellectual honesty) threatened by opacity under AI production

- Attribution becomes epistemically necessary, not merely ethically required

- Process disclosure analogous to traditional philosophy's self-critical practices

- Three principles from Section VII implement traditional values under AI-mediated conditions

  

**Key moves**:

- Establishes values before requirements

- Prevents misreading (not surveillance, not technical optimization)

- Connects transparency to preservation of what philosophy always valued

  

## The Transparency Framework

  

**Structure**: Abstract concept then concrete implementation

  

**Three disclosure components**:

1. **Model and process information**: AI system used, temporal context, role map by section

2. **Representative prompts and outputs**: Author's inputs and what they worked with (not exhaustive logs)

3. **Process narrative**: Reflective account of intellectual journey

  

**Three functions served simultaneously**:

- Verification (authorship, accountability)

- Methodological learning (community understanding)

- Preservation of traditional values (attribution, guided thought, thinking quality assessment)

  

**Concrete implementation**: This paper's supplementary materials

- Claude Sonnet 4.5, Q3-Q4 2025

- Complete synthesized prompt

- Exploratory conversation excerpts

- Section writing documentation

- Reflective account

- Appendix provides charts/diagrams showing relationships

  

**Accessibility emphasized**: Text files, conversation excerpts, reflective writing—no technical expertise required; non-technical philosophers can produce similar materials

  

## Experimental Development and Community Evolution

  

**Key claims**:

- Framework is sketch requiring experimentation, not prescription

- Venue's early phase functions as exploratory search

- Community life itself becomes trial and error

- May converge on one model or develop several viable approaches

- This paper demonstrates proof-of-concept, not prescriptive requirement

  

**Alignment with Level 2**: Venue serves philosophers doing philosophy; methodological development proceeds through philosophical practice, not imposed standardization

  

## From Transparency to Sufficiency

  

**Connection established**: Disclosure requirements create materials enabling reproduction test

  

**Reproduction test question**: Were author's documented inputs sufficient to generate the intellectual contribution?

- Not whether AI could independently produce work

- Whether author's specific prompts/guidance functionally determined contribution

  

**Dual purposes of reproduction**:

1. Verification: Guards against strategic underreporting or fabricated accounts

2. Authorship anchoring: Demonstrates author controlled "the difference that made the difference"

  

**Forward reference**: Section IX explains how reviewers conduct test, what counts as successful reproduction, how reproduction integrates with quality evaluation
